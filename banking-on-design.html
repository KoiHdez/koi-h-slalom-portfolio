Banking on Design

How a holistic design system for a global investment bank unified multiple design pods and left a governance framework the client could run independently.

Role

Lead Designer, Design Systems Pod

Client

Global investment bank

Industry

Financial Services

Design Systems UI Design

Overview

A global investment bank needed to re-envision the call center agent experience across a wide range of products. The goal: centralize a service offering that spanned multiple product lines, agent workflows, and data platforms into a unified platform — a modular dashboard where agents could interact with data providers without switching contexts.

I led the design system effort at the center of that work. Multiple pods were working in parallel on different project streams, and without a shared foundation, the risk of divergence was high. My job was to build the system that kept them coherent — and then make sure the client could maintain it after we left.

Problem

The client's "North Star" vision was ambitious: a single, unified platform where call center agents could access their full product suite without friction. But the path to get there required multiple design pods working on different parts of the experience simultaneously — and inconsistent local decisions compound quickly at that scale.

There was no shared design foundation. Each pod was making independent decisions about components, patterns, and interaction models. The work needed a system that could accommodate the unique needs of each pod while keeping the end-to-end experience coherent.

My Role

As Lead Designer for the Design Systems Pod, I led the creation of the design system and provided leadership across pods to maintain coherence across project streams. I coordinated with the client's development teams to ensure timely component delivery and facilitated cross-program collaboration — learning the unique needs of each pod and translating those into shared system decisions.

I acted as the design system subject matter expert throughout the engagement, partnering closely with the client's design leads. That partnership was critical: the system wasn't just for the duration of the engagement — it needed to be something the client could own and evolve independently.

Process

How the work unfolded.

01 — Discovery & Cross-Pod Alignment

Before building anything, I spent time with each pod to understand the unique demands of their project streams. The audit revealed where teams had already made diverging decisions — spacing, component naming, interaction patterns — and surfaced the places where alignment was most urgent.

The goal wasn't to centralize everything. Some differences were appropriate. The goal was to agree on what had to be shared and what could stay local — and to make that distinction explicit rather than leaving it to chance.

02 — Architecture & System Design

I designed the system architecture to support the modular nature of the product — components that could be composed into different dashboard configurations without breaking. The system needed to be flexible enough for each pod's needs while rigid enough to produce a coherent end product.

I worked closely with the client's development teams throughout this phase, aligning on component contracts early so that design and engineering decisions could move in lockstep rather than creating rework at handoff.

03 — Component Build & Documentation

Components were sequenced by what was blocking active pod work, not by category or alphabetical order. I tracked requests across all pods in a shared backlog and made sequencing decisions that minimized blockers across the program. Documentation was part of the definition of done on every component — not a post-build activity.

Every component shipped with usage guidance and a clear spec for developers. That discipline kept the system trustworthy — teams used it because it was reliable, not because they were required to.

04 — Governance & Client Independence

The final phase was about making the system self-sustaining after the engagement ended. I built out content and governance guidelines covering contribution processes, versioning, deprecation policy, and decision criteria for promoting components from product libraries to the shared system.

We ran working sessions with the client's internal design leads to walk through governance in practice. The goal was for them to walk away with judgment, not just documentation — the ability to make the same decisions independently that I'd been making throughout the engagement.

The result was a mature, independently operable design system — rare for a consulting engagement. The client walked away with a comprehensive library of design assets, requirements, and governance practices. The system significantly strengthened collaboration across business units, optimized asset organization, and enabled a cohesive, frictionless digital experience.

More importantly: the team didn't need us anymore. That was the measure of success.

Reflection

The hardest part of this project wasn't the design work — it was the alignment work that had to happen before the design work could mean anything. A design system in a multi-pod program is as much an organizational intervention as it is a design artifact. The governance phase taught me that the most valuable thing you can leave behind isn't components — it's the ability for a team to make good decisions without you.
