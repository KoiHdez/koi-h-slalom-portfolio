Elevating Design Efficiency

How a unified Figma component ecosystem — anchored by an innovative Form Builder — cut time-to-design in half for a leading financial services institution.

Overview

Designers across multiple teams at a leading financial services institution lacked a centralized, reliable resource for building new experiences. The result was predictable: inconsistencies, duplicated effort, and slow delivery — particularly for card-related experiences, which were core to the product.

I led the creation of a global Figma library shared across three teams and a tailored design ecosystem for my own pod — giving designers a launchpad for rapid prototyping and a single source of truth for the current state of their products.

Problem

Every new feature started from scratch. Components existed in isolation across team files, documentation was sparse, and the card experience — a high-frequency surface — had no standardized patterns. Designers were spending more time recreating existing work than building new experiences.

The client needed a system that could serve both as a creative launchpad and a definitive source of truth. Not just components, but a foundation that designers, developers, and PMs could all trust to reflect the current state of their products.

My Role

I led the creation of the global Figma library and built the tailored design ecosystem for my team. This included full documentation of all card experiences, close collaboration with developers and product managers to ensure alignment and usability, and the introduction of new ways of working — Figma variables for content-switching and branching for version control and maintenance.

Beyond the build, I facilitated the adoption of the system across teams, running sessions to onboard designers and demonstrate the capabilities of tools like Form Builder. Getting people to use a design system is a different challenge than building one — and both mattered equally here.

Process

How the work unfolded.

01 — Audit & Alignment

I started by cataloguing what existed across team files — components, patterns, and the card experiences that were most in need of standardization. The audit made the problem visible and gave the team a shared starting point: here's what we have, here's where it diverges, here's what we'll consolidate.

Alignment with developers and PMs happened early. The system only works if it reflects what's actually buildable — so I worked to establish shared vocabulary and component contracts before any major build decisions were made.

02 — Architecture & Library Structure

The 5-library architecture separated concerns cleanly: a foundation library for core patterns and templates, a template library for reusable design modules, a full experience library capturing end-to-end customer journeys, and two additional libraries for specialized use cases and team-specific needs.

I introduced Figma variables to enable content-switching within components — a newer capability at the time that required both build investment and team education. The payoff was a system that didn't require rebuilding a component to see it in a different content state.

5-library architecture — placeholder for actual artifact

03 — Component Build & Form Builder

Components were sequenced by team demand. I tracked requests across all three teams and prioritized the build around what was actively blocking work. The standout delivery was Form Builder — a component that let designers construct form flows rapidly without rebuilding from scratch each time.

Every component shipped with documentation: usage guidance, do/don't examples, and token references. Documentation wasn't deferred to the end — it was part of the definition of done for every component in the system.

Form Builder component — placeholder for actual artifact

04 — Governance & Adoption

I introduced branching for version control and maintenance — giving teams a safe way to propose changes without destabilizing the shared library. This changed the contribution dynamic: instead of workarounds and local overrides, teams had a legitimate path to evolve the system.

Adoption was driven by demonstrating value rather than mandating use. Form Builder was the clearest proof point — once designers used it, the system sold itself.

Reflection

The most important decision on this project was introducing Figma variables early, despite the added complexity. It was a bet on the long-term usability of the system over the short-term convenience of a simpler build. That tradeoff paid off — but it required committing to education and enablement alongside the build itself. A design system no one understands isn't a design system; it's a liability.
