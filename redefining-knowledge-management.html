Redefining Knowledge Management

An internal knowledge hub for a Big Tech client — satisfaction ratings moved from 2 to 4.3 out of 5.

Overview

A leading global technology company's internal teams were drowning in fragmented knowledge systems. Information lived in silos, effort was being duplicated across departments, and employees couldn't find what they needed when they needed it. I led the UX and human-centered design work to unify these systems into a single, usable solutions hub — and rebuilt user satisfaction from a 2 to a 4.3 out of 5 in the process.

Problem

Internal teams were maintaining multiple overlapping knowledge repositories with no clear ownership. When employees needed answers, they didn't know where to look — so they asked colleagues, which meant the same questions were being answered over and over. The cost wasn't just inefficiency; it was organizational knowledge that existed only in people's heads and would leave when they did.

The existing tools scored a 2 out of 5 in user satisfaction surveys. People weren't just dissatisfied — they had stopped trusting the system to have what they needed, which became a self-fulfilling prophecy as contributions dropped.

My Role

I led the UX design and human-centered design work end-to-end — from discovery and stakeholder alignment through wireframes, high-fidelity design, and usability testing. I also worked with the client's internal team to establish the new visual standards that would carry forward beyond the initial engagement.

Process

From fragmented to unified.

01 — Discovery & Research

I started by mapping the existing landscape — every tool, every repository, every workaround employees had built to compensate for the gaps in the official system. The map was more fragmented than anyone had realized. Seeing it all together was the first catalyst for alignment.

Interviews with employees across departments revealed a consistent theme: the problem wasn't just findability, it was trust. People didn't search the knowledge base because they'd learned it wouldn't have what they needed. Redesigning the interface wasn't enough — we needed to redesign the content strategy too.

02 — Define & Prioritize

The research pointed to a unified solutions hub as the right structural answer — one place, clearly organized, with a content model that made it easy to contribute and easy to find. I worked with stakeholders to align on this direction, using the research to navigate the competing priorities different teams had brought to the table.

Stakeholder alignment on this project was as much a design challenge as the UX work itself. Different teams had different definitions of "good" and different ideas about what the hub should prioritize. I used structured workshops to surface the disagreements explicitly and work through them, rather than letting them resurface as scope changes mid-build.

03 — Design & Iterate

I moved through wireframes to high-fidelity in tight iteration cycles, testing assumptions at each stage rather than waiting for a polished prototype. The navigation architecture went through three major revisions based on usability findings — each one measurably improving task completion rates in unmoderated testing.

I also designed a CMS interface that made it easier for content owners to contribute without needing technical help. Reducing the barrier to contribution was as important as improving findability — the two problems were connected.

04 — Usability Testing & Handoff

I ran moderated usability sessions with employees across seniority levels and departments. The sessions confirmed the structural decisions held up under real use, and surfaced a handful of interaction patterns that needed refinement before launch.

Handoff included a new visual standard for the hub — documented in a lightweight style guide that the client's internal team could maintain and extend. Setting that standard was part of the brief, and it outlasted the engagement.

User satisfaction ratings moved from 2 to 4.3 out of 5 — a shift that reflected not just improved usability, but restored trust in the system as a reliable resource. Repetitive task requests dropped as employees found answers independently rather than asking colleagues.

The new visual standard I established became the baseline for the client's subsequent internal tool work — a signal that the design decisions had earned enough confidence to be extended, not replaced.

Reflection

The insight I keep coming back to from this project is that the content problem and the design problem were inseparable. A well-designed interface over untrustworthy content wouldn't have moved the needle. We had to address both — which meant the engagement had to be broader than a typical UX redesign. I'd push for that scope explicitly from the start in a future engagement, rather than arriving at it through the research.
