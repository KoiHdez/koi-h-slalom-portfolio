Life Sciences Marketplace

A marketplace built from zero that serves 7,000+ independent pharmacies and closes a $1.5B revenue gap.

Role

Product Designer, UX Designer

Client

Fortune 100 pharmaceutical org

Industry

Life Sciences

Duration

12+ months

Disciplines

Product Design UX eCommerce

Overview

A Fortune 100 pharmaceutical solutions organization was losing $1.5B annually to customers buying front-of-house products from competitors. The fix wasn't more salespeople — it was a marketplace. I led the core product design from the initial brief through MVP launch in July 2023, and continued into post-MVP improvements that deepened the platform's capability.

7,000+ independent pharmacies now use the platform. The marketplace addressed an immediate revenue gap and laid the foundation for new business models the client is still building on.

Problem

Independent pharmacies were buying front-of-house products — the everyday items that sit at the counter, not in the dispensary — from third-party suppliers instead of the client. The client had the inventory and the relationships, but no digital channel to sell through. Every order that went elsewhere was revenue that shouldn't have been lost.

The problem wasn't awareness — it was friction. Pharmacies wanted a better buying experience. Building one meant designing for buyers with highly variable technical literacy, across a product catalog with thousands of SKUs, for a client with minimal existing eCommerce infrastructure to build on.

My Role

I owned the core marketplace design — product browsing, catalog, cart, and order management. I led 10 pre-launch user interviews with independent pharmacy buyers, partnered directly with the Product Owner on feature prioritization, and worked alongside developers to close the gap between design intent and implementation.

I also contributed to team-wide design process improvements and ran the Design QA workflow pre-launch, catching integration issues that would have shipped otherwise.

Process

From blank canvas to MVP in 12 months.

01 — Discovery & Research

Before any wireframes, I ran 10 user interviews with independent pharmacy buyers. The goal was to understand their current ordering workflow and where the friction was — not to validate a solution we'd already sketched. What we heard shaped the information architecture more than any competitive analysis could have.

I supplemented the primary research with Baymard Institute's eCommerce UX research and a competitive review of B2B marketplace patterns. The combination gave us research-backed rationale for every major structural decision.

02 — Define & Prioritize

With research in hand, I worked with the Product Owner to define the MVP scope. The tension was real: the client wanted everything, the timeline demanded choices. I used the research to make the case for what to cut — features that felt important but that users hadn't asked for — and what to keep, even when it was harder to build.

I documented backend integration flows for the development team, bridging the gap between the business logic and the user experience. These became a shared reference that reduced back-and-forth significantly through build.

Integration flow diagrams — placeholder for actual artifact

03 — Design & Iterate

Design happened in Agile sprints. I worked in tight loops with the development team — not handing off finished specs, but collaborating on implementation as the designs evolved. This meant accepting some constraints early and pushing back on others where the UX impact was too significant to compromise.

The product card went through the most iteration. The MVP version was functional but exposed friction in how buyers compared products. Post-MVP, I redesigned it with richer information hierarchy — a change that measurably reduced time-to-add-to-cart in follow-up testing.

Product card MVP vs. post-MVP — placeholder for actual artifact

04 — Validation & Testing

Pre-launch, I ran Design QA against the live build — comparing implementation against design specs and documenting every deviation. Some were acceptable trade-offs; others were bugs that would have shipped. The QA process caught interaction issues that automated testing missed because they were judgment calls, not technical failures.

MVP shipped

The marketplace launched on schedule in July 2023 and onboarded 7,000+ independent pharmacy customers. The revenue gap it was built to close began closing immediately.

Post-launch, I continued on the engagement through post-MVP improvements — refining the product card, improving search and filter UX, and deepening the order management experience. The platform is still growing. The design foundation it was built on has scaled with it.

Reflection

The most useful thing I did on this project wasn't a design decision — it was insisting on user interviews before wireframes. The pressure to move fast was real, and doing research first felt like it was slowing things down. It wasn't. The interviews gave us a shared language with the client for talking about user needs, which made every subsequent prioritization conversation faster and less contentious. I'd make the same call again.
